2007
October 2007, According to the data monitoring group Nielsen Monitor-Plus, Nintendo has spent a lofty USD $40 million on print, TV and outdoor media advertising between January and June 2007. This first-half budget is about on par with what Nintendo's marketing budget has been two years' prior; with 2006's allotment totaling $86 million, and 2005's being $84 million.

Competitors: Sony and Microsoft both spent GBP 4 million (about US $8 million) for holiday marketing in the UK.

2006
In November, Nintendo announced it would spend more than $200 million marketing the Wii over the next year.

Europe: Nintendo Europe spent 35 million Euros across Europe to advertise the Nintendo GameCube and Game Boy Advance SP for the 2003 holiday season. The March 28, 2003, European launch of the Game Boy Advance SP alone received 10 million Euros across Europe.

In March, a 5 million Euro marketing campaign was launched for the release of Metroid Prime on March 21st. The campaign included a high profile MTV sponsorship deal and TV and print advertising, in addition to a limited edition Metroid Pak (a limited edition GameCube priced at 199 Euros, containingthe Metroid Prime game, along with a black Nintendo GameCube and Metroid Prime name-plate for the top of the console).

media/marketing-metroidcubepack.jpg

The highly popular MTV Winterjam 2003 held in Davos Switzerland in March, was sponsored by Metroid Prime. A competition was held at Winterjam, where the game-play was projected onto a huge 'ice wall'. The print campaign was said to be the largest ever UK campaign for a Nintendo GameCube title.

2002

North America: On October 7, 2002, Nintendo announced it was prepared to spend 25 percent more to market the GameCube towards Teens and 20-Somethings for the 2002 holiday shopping season. The $140 million marketing push would include GameCube exclusive software, such as Resident Evil 0 and Metroid Prime, along with the 80 other games that were scheduled to be released bewteen October and the end of that year. In total, 60 percent of Nintendo's global marketing budget was spent in the last four months of 2002.

Other marketing venues included in the budget are listed as follows:

  • Exclusive Nintendo Cube Clubs in urban storefronts in 15 cities, featuring live music and extensive hands-on game play
  • A celebrity-studded anniversary party for Nintendo GameCube in Hollywood
  • Intensified advertising campaigns aimed at teens and young adults highlighting a wide range of older-appeal titles from Nintendo and independent third-party publishers
  • More than 3 million promotional DVDs with 80-plus minutes of game footage representing more than 25 new games
  • Cinema advertising in Nov. and Dec. on more than 6000 screens nationwide
  • Sponsorship of the USSA Snowboarding Grand Prix, running from December through April
  • Promotional partnerships with Heineken, Keebler and Kraft Easy Mac & Cheese
  • Multi-market radio promotions
  • Intensified internet and print advertising
  • 1.5 million copies of a new 80-page, full color consumer booklet at thousands of retail outlets in November
  • Numerous out-of-home programs - in schools, malls, college campuses, airports, bars and nightclubs - reaching teens and young adults where they hang out

This year also saw Nintendo partnering with S&G Flowers to promote a new breed of flower similar to those seen in <i&rt;Pikmin</i&rt; for the GameCube. The bushy, little white flowers are technically called Bacopa Cabana, a member of the plant genus Sutera, but Nintendo paid to have the flower nicknamed - The Pikmin Flower.

Europe: This year, Nintendo Europe allocated 100 million Euros to market the launches of the Game Boy Advance and GameCube across Europe.

Competitors: For comparison, Sega spent $35 million during its marketing campaign for its Sega Sports series.

2001

North America: For the North American launch of the Game Boy Advance on June 11, Nintendo spent between $25-30 million. Nintendo would spend a total of $75 million by the end of the year. Game Boy and GameCube marketing expenses would total $150 million.

Game Boy Advance marketing plans called for three 30-second commercials that would air in the weeks leading up to, and during the June 11th launch. The campaign, called "Life Advanced", told players to live 'Advanced' - in the place where fantasy and reality collide.

Also, in an attempt to build awareness of the handheld, Nintendo sponsored a mobile tour showcasing the Game Boy Advance in 11 North American locations. Additionally, retailers around the country featured Game Boy Advance kiosks. Finally, select high schools and junior high schools featured Game Boy Advance advertisements.

For the eight weeks running up to and following the North American GameCube launch on November 18, Nintendo announced it would spend nearly $75 million. Nintendo dubbed the November 18 launch as "Cube Sunday".

The campaign was targetted at all gamers. Five exclusive first- and second-party games and more than 10 third-party games, for a total of 20 exclusive titles were part of the marketing campaign. The campaign also included:

  • Seven different television commercials aired on stations from MTV to Saturday morning cartoons (Produced by directory of photography from movies such as "Seven" and "The Beach".) One of the commercials featured more than 500 cast members, adding to the epic scope of the television advertising campaign.
  • Previews aired before movies such as Harry Potter and Lord of the Rings.
  • Print advertisements in magazines such as Sports Illustrated for Kids, Maxim and Stuff. More than 95 full-page print ads appeared in more than 50 publications.
  • Out-of-home advertising included in-school and mall dioramas, Channel One Broadcasting advertising, and banners in malls across the United States and more.
  • Nintendo Cube Clubs kicked off on September 28, visiting 12 cities nationwide.
  • Internet campaign with launch of the official Nintendo GameCube Web site. National online contest asking "What Would YOU Do for a Nintendo GameCube?
  • Nintendo distributed more than 3 million sampling CDs featuring Nintendo game play footage, links to the official GameCube Web site, downloadable items and more.
  • In retailers, 10,000 Nintendo GameCube kiosks gave customers a chance to sample the system before launch, in addition to in-store advertising with banners and displays.
  • Starz Encore Group, the largest provider of cable and satellite-delivered premium movie channels in the United States, teamed up with Nintendo as part of its blockbuster Fall Free preview.
  • During the STARZ! and BLACK STARZ! Free Preview Weekend on October 5-7 viewers were given the chance to win 500 Nintendo GameCube systems along with other Nintendo prizes.
  • Dr Pepper and Nintendo collaborated on a nationwide consumer promotion to give consumers a chance to instantly win a GameCube. Dr Pepper supported the promotion with television advertising, point-of-sale and special packaging for the 20-ounce and 2-liter bottles, as well as 12-packs of 12-ounce cans.
  • Partnered with Yahoo! Instant Messenger to advertise its GameCube title "Super Smash Bros. Melee".</ul&rt;</blockquote&rt;</font&rt;</p&rt;

The campaign - aimed at the college male crowd - ran for six weeks, starting on February 19. The game launched on March 5. To advertise the game, Starcom created "Playboy's Conker the Mansion Sweepstakes". The prize was a trip to the Playboy Mansion for the Playmate of the Year party in April.

Competitors: In comparison, for the 18 months leading up to and following the launch of the Xbox console, Microsoft spent $500 million in advertising. According to Robbie Bach, senior vice president of Microsoft's games division, this was one of the largest product launches ever from the company.

1999

Japan: In January, Nintendo advertised The Legend of Zelda: Ocarina of Time within several Japanese newspapers. The portrait showed a group of people with pointy ears. The Japanese text says "Zelda population 6,000,000" meaning that The Legend of Zelda: Ocarina of Time had shipped more than 6,000,000 copies worldwide.

Competitors: For comparison, Sony Computer Entertainment America spent $150 million for its winter holiday marketing campaign.

1998

North America: Nintendo announced its total level of marketing expenditures for the fourth quarter of 1998 would be $125 million. As a part of these expenses, the Game Boy Color would receive a comprehensive TV campaign expressing the message for "gaming on the go". In addition, Game Boy ads would be displayed on billboards, bus shelters and airport lounges. This year, Nintendo spent $10 million advertising its The Legend of Zelda: The Ocarina of Time gold cartridge pre-sell campaign which ultimately generated 250,000 pre-sells. Nintendo was also advertising its Pokemon Pikachu handheld, which was launched in November and retailed for $19.95.

Nintendo 64 sales figures were expected to reach 4.5 million in the year ending March 31, with software reaching 28 million units. Nintendo also expected the Super Nintendo to sell through another 1 million hardware units and 7 million games.

A $10 million campaign was set aside for the Game Boy Color. Under the tagline "Escape To Color," TV spots from Leo Burnett, began in black-and-white showing a kid overwhelmed by work-day pressures, a gym teacher yelling at him to climb the rope and a dentist demanding him to floss. The boy then gets sucked down the dentist's spit basin and into a world of color.

Competitors: For comparison, Sega of America spent $100 million during its DreamCast marketing campaign this year. Sony meanwhile, spent $150 million in North America to counter Sega's DreamCast advertising.

1997

North America: Nintendo of America spent $7 million to market the release of StarFox 64. The marketing campaign included a a direct mail videotape shipped to nearly one million video game enthusiasts nationwide. Meanwhile, in a similar marketing campaign, Nintendo spent $20 million for the marketing release of Diddy Kong Racing - $10 million of which was spent on a cross-promotional campaign with Taco Bell. This set a record for Nintendo spending on a single piece of software. Most Nintendo 64 releases were supported by a $6-$7 million marketing campaign.

This year also saw Nintendo launching its Nintendo 64 advertising campaign that featured the tagline "Get N or Get Out!" The total fourth quarter advertising expenditures totaled $90 million. Nintendo and ad agency Leo Burnett began aiming their ads at the growing older demographic of gamers. Games highlighed included GoldenEye 007, Turok: The Dinosaur Hunter, Star Fox 64, Mario Kart 64, NFL Quarterback Club, International Superstar Soccer 64, WCW vs. NWO: World Tour and Madden 64.

Nintendo projected that 65 percent of its target base would be reached with the new holiday blitz.


Competitors: Sony spent $100 million during its North American marketing campaign for 1997. Meanwhile, Sega only put $25 million into its Saturn marketing.

1996

North America: For the North American launch of the Nintendo 64 on September 26, 1996, Nintendo together with ad agency Leo Burnett, performed a three-part series of advertisements that cost as much as $100 per unit, or $54 million in total. Nintendo partnered with Blockbuster Video and Nickelodeon for the launch.

1995

North America: Nintendo announced that supporting the North American launch of the Virtual Boy on August 14, 1995, was a more than $20 million marketing campaign. Nintendo of America projected sales of 2 million Virtual Boy hardware units and 4 million Virtual Boy software units by March of 1996.

Nintendo planned to spend $2 million in national advertising for the SNES release of Earthbound to target teens and young adults.

Competitors: Sony launched the PlayStation in North America on September 9, 1995. The console retailed for $299, however was still $100 less than the Sega Saturn. Over 100,000 units were presold at launch, and 17 games were available. Stores reported sell-outs across the country, and sold out of many games and peripherals as well, including second controllers and memory cards.

Sony's initial marketing strategy was aimed at an older audience than the traditional 8-16 year old demographic of the past. With the tag line "U R Not E" (the "E" being red) and a rumored $40 million to spend on launch marketing, Sony moved swiftly to position itself as the market leader. To further cement their audience demographic, Sony sponsored the 1995 MTV Music Awards.

1993

North America: On May 28, Super Mario Bros. makes its nationwide debut in theaters via Hollywood Pictures. The film is based on the characters featured in the Mario Bros. title including: Bowser, Princess Toadstool, Mario, Luigi and more. The film is said to have a boring plot but is saved by its stunning visual effects.

1992

North America: At the Winter Consumer Electronics Show in Las Vegas, Nevada, Nintendo of America announced its intention to boost media spending by 20 percent for the year. This would raise the number to a total of $40 million.

While not at all related to advertising expenditures, the following did get Nintendo's name in the news. This year, Nintendo president Hiroshi Yamauchi, offered to buy a 60 percent share of the Seattle Mariners baseball team and hinted that he would like to some day move Nintendos headquarters from Japan to Seattle. On Tuesday, June 9, a group of investors led by Hiroshi Yamauchi purchased the Seattle Mariners baseball club for $125 million. Yamauchis contribution was $75 million. This marked the first time in history that a U.S. major league team was allowed majority ownership outside of North America.

1991

North America: In April, Nintendo of America awards their Super Nintendo System account to the advertising agency of Leo Burnett USA. This is the agency Nintendo will build a relationship with over the next decade.

On Sunday, September 8, Nintendo of America kicked off the biggest TV campaign in history that would continue through December 29. With a $25 million advertising budget dedicated to the 16-bit Super Nintendo System (SNES) and another $10 million allocated to the NES line and the Game Boy, Nintendo had plans to buy the market away from the Sega Genesis (which was introduced in 1989).

Competitors: During the week of Monday, September 16, Sega of America kicked off its $1 million/2-day blitz of a $10 million advertising campaign depicting the Sega Genesis as the superior choice against the Nintendo 16-bit alternative. The slogan trumpeted that "Genesis Does what Nintendon't!"

1990

North America: Earthbound for the Nintendo Entertainment System was scheduled to release this year. Nintendo of America however was hesitant to release the title. They didn't feel it fit into the product mix that the market was asking for and/or expecting. One such excuse that has been given is that RPGs were not yet exceptionally popular to the North American gaming crowd. This was further compounded by Nintendo's marketing department shifting focus to the preparation of the Super Nintendo marketing campaign for 1991. Inevitably, Earthbound missed it's window to launch thereby completely losing out on a future North American release.

Japan: Nintendo Japan released the Game Boy along with flagship titles Super Mario Land, Alleyway and Baseball.
